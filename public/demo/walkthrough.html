§1 · 0:00 · premise

§1.5 · 0:25 · the disagreement

What we don't agree on yet — and what I want to spend the next six minutes on — is how to build it.

§2 · 0:45 · the five gaps

Five things, specifically.

Safety.

Trust.

Ownership.

Onboarding.

Context.

§2.5 · 1:05 · trust dressed up as setup

Anyone here who's tried one knows: the fast path is fifteen minutes, the real path is a weekend.

And the default posture is allow-all — you hand over the keys on day one and harden later, if you remember.

That's a trust problem dressed up as a setup problem.

The deeper trust problem: you can't actually run any of these with full access to your life.

You'd never let claude touch everything on your machine — claude could fuck you and delete your stuff.

You wouldn't run them while you watch reruns of Magical Girl Anime.

So you turn them on, you turn them off, you guard them.

Ownership: your context lives somewhere else.

Context: it's laborious to even create.

§3 · 1:30 · what i built

So I built solstone.

solstone is a personal agent that runs entirely on your device.

No cloud.

No accounts.

No authentication.

You install it, and it starts.

That's the whole onboarding.

It's open source.

Everything you do, everything you see, everything you say — stays on your machine.

Always.

§3.5 · 1:45 · scariest software

§4.5 · 2:30 · sol onboards you

sol — that's the agent — sits silently.

It observes everything you do on your computer.

It builds your life log in the background.

Every email, every meeting, every screen.

It's not journaling because you told it to.

It's not summarizing because you asked.

It's just observing.

Saving everything.

Then — and this is the weird part — it wakes itself up.

When it knows enough about you to be useful, it starts the conversation.

Not before.

Not when you click a button.

When it decides you're ready.

That's a different shape of software.

You don't onboard sol. sol onboards you.

§8 · 6:00 · the moat (1 + 2)

Three things make this work that nobody else is doing all three of:

One. Local-first.

Everything stays on your device.

Open source.

Nothing leaves unless you say so.

Two. Architectural alignment.

sol is designed so you don't ever turn it off.

The covenants are in the articles of incorporation — public, filed, irrevocable.

The company can never be sold.

Your data can never be sold.

Limitless raised $33 million on a privacy promise.

Meta acquired them in December.

Two months later, every promise was gone.

That can't happen here.

Not technically — legally.

§8.5 · 6:20 · the moat (3) — distillation

Three. Distillation.

Your sol is yours.

The agents you build from it inherit your values, your skills, your judgment — only as much as you're comfortable with.

You don't get a generic assistant.

You get you, in the loop with every other agent you'll ever work with.
